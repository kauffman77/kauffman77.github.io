Summary Statistics for Multiple Choice Questions

I'm glad to see that students felt they learned a fair amount about things related the course.

We'll plan to keep on with the EP system.

It seems the projects struck a reasonable balance of difficulty which encouraged learning. My general impression is that students could likely handle slightly more challenge in projects so long as the associated scaffolding is in place so we may up the ante a bit in the next offering. A notable exception is Project 3's puzzlebin which is discussed below.

Allowing "click-through" in HW is a bit controversial but it does create a low-stakes learning mechanism for students who want more practice while not punishing those that are short on time. We're likely to continue this practice going forwards.

The message here appears to be discussion exercises did a reasonable job of providing slightly more challenging practice exercises to students than HW while supporting them through TA assistance. According to discussions with both students and TAs, discussion exercises can be made somewhat more dense to hit more topics without stressing anyone out too much so we are likely to tweak this for the next round to give students more the chew on for discussion exercises.

A common sentiment among faculty is that if they record their lectures and make them available to students, no one will bother coming to in-person meetings. My direct experience after making recordings available since 2020 is that many students are still very interested in the live experience of being in room and learning together. All recordings do is make it easier to review or recover if one misses lecture. I'm glad to see that the student sentiment expressed here continues to directly support that view and I'll plan to continue providing lecture recordings.

I'm heartened to see that the multiple avenues we had open to provide student support did the trick.

This is a very encouraging set of results to me as forming connections during a college career has lasting effects. While an undergrad, I learned more because I met some folks with similar interests and some of those friendships have persisted over the past 20 years. For those that found it difficult to make connections, don't be discouraged. There were a variety of courses I didn't meet anyone but being persistent in your efforts to form a community will pay off in the end.

Ah GRACE… I'm glad that we had a shared server resource on which students could do their course business as the alternative is less palatable: getting students set up with virtual machines on their local machines has been extremely painful in the past. That said, GRACE could use some updates and attention which I'll be attempting to give it prior to the next course offering. The more ergonomic we can make this for students, the better.

If folks who did manage to VS Code or other systems to work with remote editing on GRACE would drop me a line, I'd appreciate learning from what tricks you used get things in order. We'd like to make those tricks public knowledge so others have an easier time in the future.

  • The entire concept of Assembly was straight up useless and served no purpose other than to piss me off.

    This reminds me of a recording of a talk at a reasonably big conference where the speaker was ranting in a similar fashion: "It's 202x, assembly is completely irrelevant, there are so way better languages, why don't they make assembly better and more like them…" Like the above commenter, it made me chuckle and tune out as the viewpoint is missing a fundamental understanding of why assembly exists. It's like a physicist saying "look at how complex quantum mechanics is; it's much easier to reason about things if we ignore quantum effects, let's do that!" It sounds silly because quantum mechanics characterizes reality pretty well. Glossing over it gets wrong answers and willfully ignores a lower-level of known truth. In computing, if you are running programs in any language on a normal computer (e.g. not paper/pencil, not imaginary, not quantum), the CPU is really executing assembly instructions. If you want this to own the "science" part of "computer science", it's best to make peace with the nature of the beast. It's fine if you wish to be frustrated when coding assembly and find ways to avoid it professionally. But being pissed off at assembly is like being pissed off at quantum mechanics, gravity, or electromagnetism: you're likely to stay pissed because they aren't going anywhere.

  • I think the open resource exams and hand-holding in the projects make the class a bit too easy. I'm not saying all the resources were bad, but if in the future I need to code a block of assembly, I would have to look things up whereas someone else might just have remembered from their coursework.

    Working backwards, I'd be quite surprised if ANY student a year from now could easily remember how to code assembly from scratch. That goes for those who had closed resource exams. There are a LOT of details to remember about C, Assembly, and systems programming. While memorizing some of it may allow recall for a short time, everyone will forget those details over time. Being able to use a resource to quickly re-acquaint oneself is a necessary skill in a computing career. That's why I favor open resource exams.

    I'm glad that you found the course easy. If you were the only student I was teaching, I could have easily upped the stakes for you. However, my job is to assess overall student understanding across the entire population of the class. So far things look to be about where I'd like them. There is a bit of refinement to do, but I need to weigh your opinion against those that stated the opposite, that the course was too hard. As they say, the sign of a good compromise is that everyone is equally unhappy.
