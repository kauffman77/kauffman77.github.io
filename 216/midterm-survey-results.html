Lecture

  • I wish that the in class lectures were post3d the same day. Sometimes, I watch them so I can write down things that I might have missed during lecture; however, the current delay of the recorded lecture videos kills all momentum for me as I'm pretty busy and might not have time to watch it the day it's released

    Videos are usually posted within 24 hours of when the lecture was given. While there are occasional delays on this, "the current delay" you mentioned does not seem to reflect this pattern.

  • I often walk out of lecture completely lost on the material that we just went over, but when I look at the labs and projects I find out that it's way simpler than I thought.

  • I feel that learning the content and getting a grasp on it in lecture is relatively easy. The part I struggle with the most is implementing it in projects

    Learn by doing. We are in a constructive field and most computing practitioners need to make things: programs, algorithms, analyses, etc. Listening someone talk about doing this can give some insight but there is not substitute for actually doing it yourself. So, just do it.

  • In class, people ask too many random questions that distract from the course just for the sake of participation.

  • I love the interactive nature of the lectures.

    I favor dialogue in our meetings. There is a modest cost to this in that some students may find the questions asked by others to be irrelevant. I've not found that to be the case much. Options in the above numerical results split somewhat on this with about 34% of students indicating they learn from the questions of others and 24% indicating they find student questions detract from the discussion. I'll always do my best to keep the presentation on track but questions from students are an important way to broaden and deepen the topics we discuss so I'll continue to field them frequently.

  • Lectures feel a bit slow and it's working better for me to learn almost all the material from the labs/hws/projects.

    Choose your own adventure: if it works better for you to skip lecture, by all means do so. We've set up what we hope is a rich set of resources and it's fully expected that different students will gravitate towards the coursework they feel helps them the most.

  • The main thing that I struggle with for this class is the fact that lectures go over a ton of ways to transform binary etc and don't spend a lot of time going through how to find bugs and memory problems.

  • I'm struggling with interpreting Valgrind errors still…

    I've been meaning to do a Debugging video to talk in more detail about tactics to debug C programs. I'll take this as more motivation to do so. We did discuss Valgrind basics early on but many students could probably benefit from a longer, optional treatment.

  • Sometimes I hear people around me telling each other which questions to ask so that they all get the point

    I'll ask about this on the end of the semester survey. I don't believe the "EP farming" strategy to be that wide-spread but I'm curious about it.

  • Lecture notes are a little hard to follow at times because there are large amounts of text to read.

  • I don't really know if I should take notes during lecture because the slides are very detailed already.

    I am guilty of plopping down lots of text on my slides. From a strict presentation standpoint, this is a no-no. However, I learned early in my teaching career that my slides are NOT just used for presentations and are rather a study tool students use for a variety of things including out-of-class review. Making them reasonably self-contained with more dense information became more important after realizing this. Re-explaining something in one's own words via notes is ALWAYS a good idea. The act of formulating and writing such notes will help to internalize the information even if you never look at the notes again.

Exposure to C will make you a better programmer. You'll start to see as you code in Java "oh, this is a memory allocation, this is actually a pointer, this will be in the Stack and this in the Heap, etc." So too with all other languages. Getting used to C and what has come after it (Lisp, Java, Python, OCaml, Rust, etc.) will give you perspective and allow you to select the right tool (programming language) for the job. But, it does take time to learn the tool and its quirks.

Potpourri

  • Since I've had some previous exposure to concepts introduced in this class, like how memory works, binary representations of data, text encodings, and files, I've had a fairly easy time grasping the material taught in class, and C hasn't proved too difficult either.
  • For some reason always feel like most students are already pros at this stuff while I am just being introduced to these things for the first time.

  • I'm not fully comfortable navigating UNIX or creating programs that utilize the command line yet.

    A large class like this one will have student with a wide range of backgrounds. Some will have significant experience with many of the concepts, others none. DO NOT LET THIS INTIMIDATE YOU. This course is designed to expose everyone to low-level systems and supposed "pros" are likely those that have done some of this before. Just keep walking your own path and you'll make progress. There are a lot of things to learn in a course like this so absorb what you can knowing that these things will be around for your entire career and you can always round back to them.

  • I think it I necessary to have grades and feedback as soon as possible within the course, not to be weeks in with assignments done and no grades or understanding of where I am at as far as grade/feedback.

    Grades for HWs and Labs are available immediately. I'm not sure how much faster we can get. Project tests results are also available immediately. Project manual inspection and grades are now available at the beginning of Week 7 of the course. If you are concerned about your standing in the course, use the posted grade calculator to tabulate or email Prof Kauffman to talk it over.

  • The introduction of file reading, command line, and other dynamic user input that is very different from object orientated to be pretty difficult to wrap my head around

    While there may be some surface syntax differences, file I/O looks about the same in all languages. A good demo of this would be to port Banlet to Java or Python. You'll see that there are the same basic "get stuff from this file" calls there though they'll likely return you a String instead in those contexts.

    It feels weird now, but another few months and it will feel normal. In that time you'll go from looking things up frequently, to less frequently, to rarely. That is the pattern of learning anything, whether it's your lines in a play, how kick a ball, or the fingerings for notes on a violin. Keep practicing and you'll own it eventually.
